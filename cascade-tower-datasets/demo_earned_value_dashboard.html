Cumulative S-Curve ? What is this?

Planned Value (BCWS) vs Earned Value (BCWP) vs Actual Cost (ACWP) — cumulative CAD

How to read the S-Curve

The S-Curve is the most fundamental EVM visualization. It plots three cumulative cost curves over time. The blue line (BCWS) is your baseline plan — how much value you should have earned by each month. The green line (BCWP) is how much value you actually earned. The red line (ACWP) is how much you actually spent.

Key takeaway: On Cascade Tower, the green line (earned) has fallen consistently below the blue line (planned) since Month 6 — this is the schedule variance gap caused by the facade supplier failure. Meanwhile, the red line (actual cost) runs above the green line — meaning we're spending more than we're earning, a cost overrun of $3.6M. Both gaps opened in Month 6 and have not recovered.

Schedule Performance Index (SPI) ?

Monthly and cumulative — target is 1.00

What SPI tells you

SPI = Earned Value / Planned Value. An SPI of 1.00 means on schedule. Below 1.00 = behind, above = ahead. The monthly SPI (solid purple) shows that period's efficiency, while the cumulative SPI (dashed blue) shows the overall project trend.

Key takeaway: Two sharp SPI drops stand out — Month 6 (SPI 0.85) when the facade QA failure hit, and Month 11 (SPI 0.81) when the new facade actually arrived 3 weeks late. The cumulative SPI has settled at 0.92, meaning Cascade Tower is earning value at 92% of the planned rate. To finish on time, the remaining work would need to achieve an SPI of ~1.25 — which is very aggressive and unlikely without acceleration.

Cost Performance Index (CPI) ?

Monthly and cumulative — target is 1.00

What CPI tells you

CPI = Earned Value / Actual Cost. A CPI of 1.00 means every dollar spent earns a dollar of value. Below 1.00 = cost overrun, above = under budget. CPI is considered the most reliable EVM metric — research shows that after a project is 20% complete, the cumulative CPI rarely improves by more than 10%.

Key takeaway: Cascade Tower's CPI has been remarkably stable at 0.94–0.97 throughout — never crashing badly but never recovering to 1.00 either. The cumulative CPI of 0.95 means for every $1.00 spent, we only earn $0.95 of value. At 81.9% complete, this CPI is very unlikely to improve (per the "CPI stability rule"), so the project will finish over budget. EAC = BAC / CPI = $82M / 0.95 = ~$86.3M.

Monthly Schedule & Cost Variance ?

Negative = behind schedule (SV) or over budget (CV)

Reading variance bars

Schedule Variance (SV) = EV − PV. Cost Variance (CV) = EV − AC. Both are in absolute dollar terms. Negative bars mean underperformance. These are useful for identifying which specific months had the worst performance.

Key takeaway: The largest schedule variance hits are Month 11 (−$1.2M) and Month 6 (−$800K) — both facade-related events. Cost variance is more evenly distributed at −$150K to −$350K per month, suggesting a systemic 3–5% cost overrun rather than a single blowout. The consistent negative CV across all 15 months confirms this is a project-wide efficiency issue, not a one-off.

Estimate at Completion (EAC) Trend ?

Forecasted final project cost vs original budget

What EAC tells the owner

EAC is the forecasted total cost when the project finishes. It's calculated as: EAC = ACWP + (BAC − BCWP) / CPI. This assumes future work will be performed at the current cost efficiency. The dashed line is the original Budget at Completion (BAC = $82M).

Key takeaway: EAC has climbed steadily from $82.0M to $85.9M — a $3.9M (4.8%) projected overrun. The steepest jump was between Months 5–7 (facade event). Since Month 9 the EAC has risen more slowly (~$200K/month), suggesting cost performance has stabilized but not recovered. With original contingency at $4.1M (5% of BAC), the project has consumed $3.2M in contingency and has only $900K remaining for the final 3 months.

Monthly Spend Profile ?

BCWS vs BCWP vs ACWP per month — non-cumulative

Monthly cash flow pattern

This chart shows the monthly (non-cumulative) values of planned spend, earned value, and actual cost. Unlike the S-curve which shows running totals, this reveals the shape of each month's performance individually.

Key takeaway: Peak spend occurred in Months 7–9 ($6.0–6.5M/month) during the superstructure phase — which is typical for a high-rise project. The spend profile shows a clear bell curve. Notice that ACWP (red) consistently exceeds BCWP (green) every single month — confirming the systemic cost inefficiency. The monthly BCWS (blue) peaked at $6.5M in Month 8 and has since declined, which is expected as the project transitions from heavy structure/envelope work to finishes and commissioning.

How the editable table works

The three input columns — BCWS, BCWP, and ACWP — are editable. When you change a value, the entire row and all subsequent rows recalculate their derived metrics automatically. This lets you explore questions like: "What if we had earned $5.5M instead of $4.4M in June?
